The Chaos & You Series

Books

A connected body of philosophical work by Marius Johan Stokknes, writing as M. J. Stokknes.

Each book opens a different part of the machinery: power, love, time, reality, identity, information, faith, entropy, and civilization. None of them promise rescue. They make the pressure visible.

Published Volumes

Begin with the pressure you recognize.

The books can be read independently. Together, they form a philosophical anatomy of the modern human condition: the human organism living inside systems it built, inherited, internalized, and frequently mistakes for reality itself.

Power, Chaos & You

A confrontation with invisible power, internalized obedience, social fear, spectacle, algorithms, conformity, and the quiet structures shaping modern behavior. The book examines how control becomes strongest when it no longer looks like control, when people begin carrying the prison inside themselves and mistake participation for freedom.

This book will not save you.

Love, Chaos & You

An examination of intimacy in an age of endless comparison, emotional performance, digital connection, projection, loneliness, and exhaustion. The book explores what happens when the market enters the heart, when affection becomes measurable, availability becomes labor, and people begin confusing being seen with being known.

This book will not save your relationship.

Time, Chaos & You

A philosophical study of urgency, memory, aging, productivity, rest, deadlines, and the illusion of ordered experience. The book looks at how time becomes more than measurement: a quiet authority shaping guilt, ambition, fatigue, and the strange modern belief that every moment must justify itself.

This book will not save your schedule.

Explore the series on Amazon →

Reality, Chaos & You

An examination of perception, interpretation, biology, trauma, addiction, cognitive limits, and the conditions people cannot simply think their way out of. The book confronts the uncomfortable fact that understanding does not always create leverage, that reality does not respond, and that the organism still has limits no matter how clearly the machinery is seen.

This book will not make your reality — or help you escape it.

Explore the series on Amazon →

Identity, Chaos & You

A psychological and philosophical autopsy of selfhood, masks, memory, trauma, algorithms, dopamine loops, social performance, neurodivergence, and internal conflict. The book rejects the idea of a stable, singular self and examines identity as a survival strategy built from pressure, habit, fear, and the stories used to hold the structure together.

This book will not tell you who you are.

Explore the series on Amazon →

Information, Chaos & You

A study of information overload, media saturation, attention erosion, short-form content, misinformation, selective truth, language, context, algorithms, and the collapse of proportion. The book asks how humanity became more informed than ever while becoming less capable of deciding what deserves belief, attention, or meaning.

This book will not teach you which information is correct.

Explore the series on Amazon →

Faith, Chaos & You

A philosophical examination of belief after traditional certainty begins to weaken. The book explores ritual, surrender, meaning, technology, metrics, productivity, performance, and the persistent human need to feel witnessed by something larger than the self. It asks whether faith disappeared, or simply adapted.

This book will not save your faith.

Wider Series

The machinery does not stop at the published shelf.

The wider Chaos Series extends the same framework into additional domains. These books continue the larger examination of systems, decay, collective dependency, and the temporary structures people mistake for permanence.

Entropy, Chaos & You

A descent into disorder, fragmentation, decay, collapse, and the slow erosion of coherence across bodies, minds, relationships, and institutions.

Civilization, Chaos & You

An examination of institutions, infrastructure, collective dependency, order, spectacle, memory, collapse, and the systems holding modern life together temporarily.

The Lie That Someone Knows
